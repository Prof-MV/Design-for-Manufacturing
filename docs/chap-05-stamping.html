5.2 Introduction to the Stamping Process

Metal stamping is one of the most versatile and widely used manufacturing processes in modern industry. It involves placing flat sheet metal‚Äîeither in coil or blank form‚Äîinto a stamping press where a tool and die surface forms the metal into the desired shape. Stamping encompasses a variety of operations including blanking, punching, bending, drawing, embossing, and coining.

The significance of stamping lies in its ability to produce high volumes of identical parts with excellent dimensional accuracy, tight tolerances, and superior surface finish‚Äîall at relatively low cost per part. This makes stamping essential for industries ranging from automotive and aerospace to electronics and consumer appliances.

5.3 Essential Components of Stamping

Every stamping operation requires several basic elements working in harmony: 1. The Sheet Metal (Workpiece): This is the raw material, typically supplied as coils or flat sheets. Common materials include steel, aluminum, brass, copper, and stainless steel.

  1. The Die: A specialized tool that contains the negative (female) form of the part. The die is mounted on the press bed.

  2. The Punch: The male counterpart that presses the sheet metal into or through the die cavity.

  3. The Press: The machine that provides the force to drive the punch into the die. Presses can be mechanical, hydraulic, or servo-driven.

  4. The Blank Holder (in drawing operations): A component that controls material flow and prevents wrinkling during deep drawing operations.

Figure 5.1: Basic stamping setup showing punch, die, and workpiece arrangement.

5.4 Types of Stamping Operations

Stamping is not a single process but a family of related operations. Understanding each operation is crucial for designing parts that can be efficiently manufactured.

5.4.1 Blanking and Punching

Blanking and punching are shearing operations that cut sheet metal using a punch and die.

  • Blanking: The cut-out piece (the blank) is the desired part, and the remaining sheet is scrap.
  • Punching (Piercing): The cut-out piece is scrap (the slug), and the remaining sheet with the hole is the desired part.

Figure 5.2: Blanking vs.¬†Punching: In blanking, the cut piece is the product. In punching, the sheet with the hole is the product.

‚ùì Question for Reflection: A manufacturer needs to create both circular washers and sheets with circular holes for ventilation. Which operation would they use for each product?

Click to reveal the answer
  • Washers: Blanking‚Äîthe circular cut-out is the desired product.
  • Ventilation sheets: Punching‚Äîthe sheet with holes is the desired product, and the circular slugs are scrap.

5.4.2 Bending

Bending is a forming operation where the sheet metal is deformed along a straight axis to create an angled shape. The metal on the outside of the bend is stretched (in tension), while the metal on the inside is compressed.

Figure 5.3: V-bending operation showing the punch, die, and bent workpiece.

Key bending terminology:

Term Definition
Bend Radius The inside radius of the bend
Bend Angle The angle of the bend measured from the original flat position
Springback The elastic recovery of the material after the bending force is removed
Bend Allowance The arc length of the neutral axis in the bend region
K-Factor The ratio of the neutral axis location to the material thickness

ü§î Critical Thinking: Why do you think the neutral axis (where there is neither tension nor compression) is NOT exactly at the center of the sheet thickness during bending?

5.4.3 Drawing

Drawing is a process that transforms a flat sheet metal blank into a hollow, three-dimensional shape (like a cup or box) by pulling the material into a die cavity with a punch.

Figure 5.4: Deep drawing process showing blank transformation into a cup shape.

The distinction between shallow drawing and deep drawing is based on the ratio of drawing depth to diameter:

\[\text{Drawing Ratio} = \frac{D_b}{D_p}\]

Where:

  • \(D_b\) = Diameter of the original blank
  • \(D_p\) = Diameter of the punch (final part)

When the drawing ratio exceeds approximately 2.0, the operation typically requires multiple drawing stages (redrawing) to avoid failure.

‚ùì Calculation Challenge: A beverage can manufacturer wants to draw a can body with a 66 mm diameter from a circular blank. If the maximum single-draw ratio is 2.0, what is the minimum blank diameter that can be used in a single drawing operation?

Click to reveal the solution

Using the drawing ratio formula:

\[D_b = D_p \times \text{Drawing Ratio} = 66 \text{ mm} \times 2.0 = 132 \text{ mm}\]

The maximum blank diameter for a single draw operation is 132 mm. Any larger blank would require multiple drawing stages.

5.4.4 Embossing and Coining

  • Embossing: Creates raised or recessed designs in sheet metal without changing the material thickness significantly. One side has a raised pattern while the other has a corresponding depression.

  • Coining: A severe squeezing operation that creates fine detail by forcing metal to flow into die cavities under very high pressure. Unlike embossing, coining can significantly change local thickness.

Figure 5.5: Comparison of embossing (left) and coining (right) operations.

ü§î Think About It: Why are coins made using the coining process rather than embossing? Consider factors like detail, durability, and counterfeit prevention.

5.4.6 Progressive Die Stamping

Progressive die stamping is a highly efficient method where a coil of sheet metal is fed through a series of stations, each performing a different operation. The part remains attached to a carrier strip until the final station, where it is cut free.

Figure 5.6: Progressive die stamping: multiple operations performed sequentially as the strip advances through the die.

Advantages of Progressive Dies:

  • Extremely high production rates (hundreds of parts per minute)
  • Multiple operations in a single press stroke
  • Reduced handling and labor costs
  • Consistent part quality

Aspect Advantages Limitations

5.6 Material Considerations for Stamping

Not all materials are equally suitable for stamping. The key material properties that influence stampability include:

5.6.1 Formability

Formability describes how much a material can be deformed before it cracks or tears. It is influenced by:

  • Ductility: The ability to undergo plastic deformation without fracture
  • Work Hardening Rate (n-value): How quickly the material strengthens as it deforms
  • Anisotropy (r-value): Directional differences in properties due to rolling direction

Figure 5.7: Forming Limit Diagram (FLD) showing safe and failure zones for different strain combinations.

The Forming Limit Diagram (FLD) is a critical tool that shows the combination of major and minor strains a material can withstand before failure.

5.6.2 Common Stamping Materials

Material Formability Typical Applications Special Considerations
Low Carbon Steel Excellent Automotive body panels, appliances Most economical; good for deep drawing
Stainless Steel Good to Moderate Kitchen equipment, medical devices Work hardens rapidly; requires more force
Aluminum Alloys Good Aerospace, beverage cans Springback issues; galling tendency
Brass Excellent Electrical connectors, decorative items Expensive but forms easily
Copper Excellent Electrical components Very ductile but soft

  4. Low Carbon Steel - Poor corrosion resistance unless coated/painted; would require additional finishing

The final choice depends on budget constraints and specific environmental conditions.

5.7 Design Guidelines for Stamping

Designing parts for efficient stamping requires understanding the capabilities and limitations of the process. Following these guidelines will result in lower costs, better quality, and faster production.

5.7.1 Hole Design

5.7.1.1 Minimum Hole Size

The minimum hole diameter should be at least equal to the material thickness to prevent punch breakage.

\[d_{min} \geq t\]

Where:

  • \(d_{min}\) = Minimum hole diameter
  • \(t\) = Material thickness

For non-circular holes, the minimum dimension should follow the same rule.

Figure 5.8: Minimum hole size relative to material thickness.

5.7.1.2 Hole Spacing

Holes should be spaced adequately apart to prevent distortion and ensure die strength:

  • Hole to hole: Minimum spacing = 2√ó material thickness
  • Hole to edge: Minimum distance = 1√ó material thickness (preferably 1.5√ó)

Figure 5.9: Recommended hole spacing guidelines.

5.7.2 Bend Design

5.7.2.1 Minimum Bend Radius

The minimum inside bend radius depends on material type, thickness, and grain direction. Bending perpendicular to the grain direction allows tighter radii.

Material Minimum Bend Radius (√ó thickness)
Soft aluminum 0 - 0.5t
Low carbon steel 0.5 - 1.0t
Stainless steel 0.5 - 1.0t
Brass (soft) 0 - 0.5t
Spring steel 2.0 - 4.0t

Figure 5.10: Bend radius and its relationship to material cracking.

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: Specifying a bend radius that is too small will result in cracking on the outer surface of the bend. Always check material specifications!

5.7.2.2 Springback Compensation

All materials exhibit springback‚Äîthe tendency to partially return to their original shape after bending. Designers must account for this:

  • Typical springback angles: 2¬∞ to 10¬∞ depending on material
  • Solutions: Over-bend the part, use bottoming dies, or specify springback allowance

Figure 5.11: Springback phenomenon: the part opens up slightly after the bending force is removed.

ü§î Engineering Challenge: A bracket requires a precise 90¬∞ bend. If the material has 5¬∞ of springback, what angle should the die produce to achieve the final 90¬∞ angle?

Click to reveal solution

The die should over-bend the material by the springback amount:

\[\text{Die angle} = 90¬∞ + 5¬∞ = 95¬∞\]

The die should bend the part to 95¬∞ so that after springback, the final angle is 90¬∞.

5.7.2.3 Bend Relief

When a bend line intersects or comes close to an edge, bend relief cuts or notches are required to prevent tearing.

Figure 5.12: Bend relief prevents tearing at the intersection of bends and edges.

Bend relief guidelines:

  • Width of relief ‚â• material thickness
  • Length of relief ‚â• material thickness + bend radius

5.7.3 Drawing Design

5.7.3.1 Draw Depth Limits

The maximum depth of a single draw operation is limited by the drawing ratio. Exceeding this limit causes wrinkling, tearing, or both.

Rule of thumb: Single draw depth should not exceed 40-50% of the blank diameter for cylindrical parts.

Figure 5.13: Draw ratio limits for single-stage drawing operations.

5.7.3.2 Corner Radii in Drawn Parts

Sharp corners in drawn parts concentrate stress and cause tearing. Minimum corner radii:

  • Bottom corner radius: ‚â• 4√ó material thickness
  • Top (die entry) radius: ‚â• 4√ó material thickness
  • Wall corner radius (rectangular parts): ‚â• 3√ó material thickness

Figure 5.14: Critical corner radii in drawn parts.

5.7.4 General Design Best Practices

5.7.4.1 Uniform Wall Thickness

Maintain uniform wall thickness throughout the part when possible. Significant thickness variations cause uneven material flow and stress concentrations.

5.7.4.2 Avoid Sharp Corners

Replace all sharp corners with radii or chamfers:

  • Reduces stress concentration
  • Extends die life
  • Improves material flow

5.7.4.3 Design for Nesting

Consider how blanks will be nested on the sheet to minimize material waste.

Figure 5.15: Good nesting design minimizes scrap and material cost.

üí∞ Cost Impact: Material costs can represent 40-60% of total stamped part cost. A well-nested design that improves material utilization from 70% to 85% can significantly reduce part cost!

5.7.4.4 Symmetry

Design symmetrical parts whenever possible:

  • Simplifies die design
  • Improves material flow during forming
  • Reduces the chance of assembly errors

5.8 Common Stamping Defects and Prevention

Understanding potential defects helps designers avoid them through proper design.

Wrinkling Wavy, folded material, typically in flange areas Excessive compressive stress; insufficient blank holder force Increase blank holder pressure; optimize blank size; add draw beads

Possible corrective actions for flange wrinkling:

  1. Increase blank holder force - More pressure prevents the material from buckling
  2. Reduce blank diameter - Less excess material to wrinkle
  3. Add draw beads - Control material flow and increase restraining force
  4. Use better lubrication - Reduce friction and improve material flow
  5. Anneal the material - Softer material flows more easily

Problem 1: A part requires a blanked shape with 50 holes, 4 mm in diameter, arranged in a grid pattern. The material is 1.5 mm thick mild steel. What is the minimum allowable spacing between hole centers?

Problem 2: An engineer wants to draw a cylindrical cup with a 40 mm inner diameter and 60 mm depth from a single blank. Is this feasible in a single drawing operation? If not, how would you approach this?

Click for solution

For a single draw, depth typically should not exceed 40-50% of blank diameter.

Assuming 50% maximum: Maximum depth = 0.5 √ó blank diameter

The blank diameter for a 40 mm cup (using drawing ratio of 2.0) = 40 √ó 2 = 80 mm

Maximum single-draw depth ‚âà 0.5 √ó 80 = 40 mm

Since the required depth (60 mm) > maximum single-draw depth (40 mm), this cannot be done in a single operation.

Solution: Use a redrawing operation (two or more drawing stages) to achieve the full 60 mm depth.

Click for solution

Die angle = Desired angle + Springback = 90¬∞ + 6¬∞ = 96¬∞

The die should over-bend the part to 96¬∞ so that after springback, the final angle is 90¬∞.
