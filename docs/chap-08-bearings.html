8.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Classify bearing types by their operating principles and load capabilities
  2. Select appropriate ball bearing types for different applications
  3. Calculate bearing life (L10) using the basic rating life equation
  4. Apply load factors to determine equivalent dynamic bearing loads
  5. Determine required dynamic load ratings for specified life requirements

Before You Read: Every rotating machine‚Äîfrom tiny precision instruments to massive industrial equipment‚Äîrelies on bearings. What functions do you think bearings serve, and why can‚Äôt rotating shafts simply rest directly in their housings?

8.2 Introduction to Bearings

Bearings are machine elements that constrain relative motion and reduce friction between moving parts. They are fundamental to virtually every rotating machine‚Äîfrom tiny precision instruments to massive industrial equipment.

Bearings serve three primary functions:

  1. Support loads (radial and/or thrust)
  2. Reduce friction between moving surfaces
  3. Guide motion with precision and repeatability

Classification of Bearings

Bearings are broadly classified into two main categories:

Figure 8.1: Classification of bearing types.

Ball Bearings

Ball bearings are the most common type of rolling element bearing. They use spherical balls as the rolling elements, held between an inner race and an outer race.

Component Function
Inner Race Mounted on the shaft; rotates with the shaft
Outer Race Mounted in the housing; typically stationary
Rolling Elements Balls that roll between races, carrying the load
Cage (Retainer) Separates and guides the balls to prevent contact
Seals/Shields Keep lubricant in and contaminants out

8.3 Ball Bearing Life Calculations

The life of a rolling element bearing is inherently statistical‚Äîeven identical bearings under identical conditions will fail at different times due to material fatigue. The L10 life (or basic rating life) is the standard measure used in bearing selection.

The L10 Life Concept

Definition: The L10 life is the number of revolutions (or hours at a given speed) that 90% of a group of identical bearings will complete or exceed before the first evidence of fatigue failure (flaking/spalling).

In other words, 10% of bearings will fail before reaching L10 life, while 90% will survive.

Basic Life Equation

The fundamental bearing life equation is:

\[L_{10} = \left(\frac{C}{P}\right)^p \times 10^6 \text{ revolutions}\]

Where:

Symbol Description Units
\(L_{10}\) Basic rating life revolutions
\(C\) Basic dynamic load rating N or lbf
\(P\) Equivalent dynamic bearing load N or lbf
\(p\) Life exponent ‚Äî

Life Exponent Values:

  • Ball bearings: \(p = 3\)
  • Roller bearings: \(p = 10/3 \approx 3.33\)

Converting to Hours

For bearings operating at constant speed, it is more practical to express life in hours:

\[L_{10h} = \frac{L_{10}}{60 \cdot n} = \frac{1}{60 \cdot n}\left(\frac{C}{P}\right)^p \times 10^6 \text{ hours}\]

Where \(n\) is the rotational speed in RPM.

This simplifies to:

\[L_{10h} = \frac{10^6}{60 \cdot n}\left(\frac{C}{P}\right)^p\]

Equivalent Dynamic Load

When a bearing experiences both radial (\(F_r\)) and axial (\(F_a\)) loads simultaneously, an equivalent dynamic load (\(P\)) must be calculated:

\[P = X \cdot F_r + Y \cdot F_a\]

Where:

Factor Description
\(X\) Radial load factor (from bearing tables)
\(Y\) Axial load factor (from bearing tables)
\(F_r\) Applied radial load
\(F_a\) Applied axial (thrust) load

For pure radial loading (most common case): \(P = F_r\)

Adjusted Rating Life

The basic L10 equation assumes ideal operating conditions. In practice, factors such as reliability requirements, lubrication quality, and contamination affect bearing life. The adjusted rating life accounts for these factors:

\[L_{na} = a_1 \cdot a_2 \cdot a_3 \cdot L_{10}\]

Or using the modern ISO 281 formulation:

\[L_{nm} = a_1 \cdot a_{ISO} \cdot L_{10}\]

Where:

Factor Description Typical Values
\(a_1\) Life adjustment factor for reliability See table below
\(a_2\) Material factor 0.5 to 2.0 (modern steels ‚âà 1.0)
\(a_3\) Operating conditions factor 0.1 to 1.0
\(a_{ISO}\) Combined lubrication-contamination factor 0.1 to 50

8.5 Plain Bearings (Bushings)

Plain bearings, also called bushings or sleeve bearings, are the simplest form of bearing. They consist of a bearing surface with no rolling elements‚Äîthe shaft slides directly against the bearing material.

Advantages of Bushings

Advantage Explanation
Compact design No need for rolling elements; minimal radial space
Low cost Simple manufacturing; no precision balls or races
Quiet operation No rolling elements to generate noise
High load capacity Large contact area; excellent for shock loads
Self-lubricating options Polymer and sintered metal bushings need no external lubrication

8.6 PV Limits for Bushings

The PV limit is the most important design criterion for plain bearings. It represents the maximum combination of bearing pressure (P) and sliding velocity (V) that the bearing material can sustain without excessive wear or failure.

The PV Concept

\[PV = P \times V\]

Where:

Symbol Description Typical Units
\(P\) Bearing pressure psi (or MPa)
\(V\) Sliding velocity ft/min (or m/s)
\(PV\) PV factor psi¬∑ft/min (or MPa¬∑m/s)

Calculating Bearing Pressure (P)

For a journal bearing (shaft in a bushing):

\[P = \frac{F}{d \times L}\]

Where:

Symbol Description
\(F\) Radial load on the bearing (lbf or N)
\(d\) Shaft (journal) diameter (in or mm)
\(L\) Bearing length (in or mm)

Note: This is the projected area method, using \(A = d \times L\), not the cylindrical surface area.

Calculating Sliding Velocity (V)

\[V = \pi \times d \times n\]

For consistent units:

English: \[V = \frac{\pi \times d \times n}{12} \quad \text{(ft/min, where } d \text{ in inches, } n \text{ in RPM)}\]

Metric: \[V = \frac{\pi \times d \times n}{60,000} \quad \text{(m/s, where } d \text{ in mm, } n \text{ in RPM)}\]
