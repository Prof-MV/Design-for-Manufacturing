Chapter 12 Power Transmission: Couplings and Clutches

12.1 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish between couplings and clutches based on their functions
  2. Identify the three types of shaft misalignment that couplings accommodate
  3. Select appropriate coupling types (rigid vs.¬†flexible) for different applications
  4. Explain the operating principles of friction clutches
  5. Calculate clutch torque capacity and design parameters

Before You Read: Consider your car. The engine crankshaft must connect to the transmission input shaft‚Äîbut you also need to disconnect them when shifting gears or stopping. What mechanisms allow both permanent connection and controlled engagement/disengagement?

12.2 Introduction to Couplings and Clutches

In mechanical power transmission systems, we often need to connect rotating shafts together or control when power is transmitted. Couplings provide permanent or semi-permanent connections between shafts, while clutches allow controlled engagement and disengagement of power transmission.

Think About It: Consider your car. The engine crankshaft must connect to the transmission input shaft‚Äîbut you also need to disconnect them when shifting gears or stopping. The clutch pedal controls a friction clutch that engages and disengages this connection. Meanwhile, the driveshaft uses flexible couplings (universal joints) to transmit power while accommodating the suspension‚Äôs movement.

12.2.1 Why Are Couplings and Clutches Important?

Component Primary Function Key Applications
Couplings Connect two shafts permanently Motor-to-pump, gearbox connections
Clutches Engage/disengage power on demand Vehicles, machinery start/stop

Both components must handle:

  • Torque transmission ‚Äì Transfer rotational force between shafts
  • Misalignment compensation ‚Äì Accommodate imperfect shaft alignment
  • Vibration damping ‚Äì Reduce transmitted shock and vibration
  • Overload protection ‚Äì Prevent damage from excessive torque

12.3 Shaft Couplings

Shaft couplings are mechanical devices that connect two rotating shafts to transmit power. They are classified into two main categories: rigid and flexible.

12.3.1 Types of Misalignment

Before selecting a coupling, it‚Äôs essential to understand the types of shaft misalignment that may be present:

Figure 12.1: Three types of shaft misalignment that couplings must accommodate

Misalignment Type Description Typical Causes
Angular Shaft centerlines intersect at an angle Mounting surface not perpendicular
Parallel (Radial) Shaft centerlines are parallel but offset Mounting holes not aligned
Axial Shafts move apart or together along axis Thermal expansion, bearing wear

Real-World Note: In practice, misalignment is usually a combination of all three types. Good coupling selection must account for the worst-case combined misalignment.

12.4 Rigid Couplings

Rigid couplings provide a solid connection between two shafts with no allowance for misalignment. They are used only when shafts are precisely aligned and remain so during operation.

Figure 12.2: Common rigid coupling designs

12.4.1 Rigid Coupling Characteristics

Type Torque Capacity Installation Cost Use Case
Sleeve Moderate Requires shaft end access Low Light-duty, aligned shafts
Flanged High Requires axial space Moderate Heavy machinery, pumps
Clamp Moderate No end access needed Low Quick replacement

Critical Warning: Rigid couplings transmit all misalignment forces directly to bearings and shafts. Even 0.1 mm of parallel misalignment can cause severe bearing wear and premature failure. Use rigid couplings only when alignment can be guaranteed.

üîÑ Flexible Couplings

Flexible couplings accommodate misalignment while transmitting torque. They use various mechanisms to provide flexibility.

12.4.2 Elastomeric Couplings

These couplings use rubber or polymer elements to provide flexibility and damping.

Figure 12.3: Jaw coupling with elastomeric spider element

Common Elastomeric Coupling Types:

Type Flexibility Damping Misalignment Capacity Applications
Jaw/Spider Moderate Good Low-Moderate Pumps, compressors
Tire/Torus High Excellent High Heavy equipment
Sleeve Low Moderate Low Light-duty drives
Disc Low Low Moderate Precision machinery

12.4.3 Metallic Flexible Couplings

These use metal elements to provide flexibility through mechanical deflection rather than material deformation.

Figure 12.4: Common metallic flexible coupling designs

12.4.4 Metallic Coupling Comparison

Type Angular Parallel Axial Torque Speed Notes
Oldham None High Low Moderate Low Zero backlash
Gear Low-Mod None Yes Very High High Requires lubrication
Universal Joint High None None High Moderate Non-constant velocity
Disc Pack Moderate Low Yes High Very High No lubrication needed

12.5 Coupling Selection Criteria

Selecting the right coupling requires consideration of multiple factors:

12.5.1 1. Torque Requirements

The coupling must transmit the required torque with an appropriate safety factor:

\[T_{coupling} \geq T_{nominal} \times SF\]

Where the Service Factor (SF) accounts for load characteristics:

Load Type Service Factor
Uniform (electric motor to centrifugal pump) 1.0 - 1.5
Light shock (reciprocating compressor) 1.5 - 2.0
Moderate shock (machine tools) 2.0 - 2.5
Heavy shock (crushers, mills) 2.5 - 3.0+

12.5.2 2. Speed Limitations

Coupling maximum speed depends on:

  • Balance quality
  • Material strength (centrifugal forces)
  • Lubrication requirements (for gear couplings)

12.5.3 3. Misalignment Capacity

Select based on expected misalignment:

Table 12.1: Typical Misalignment Capacities by Coupling Type
Coupling Type Angular (¬∞) Parallel (mm) Axial (mm)
Rigid 0 0 0
Jaw/Spider 0.5 - 1.0 0.2 - 0.5 0.5 - 2.0
Disc Pack 1.0 - 2.0 0.1 - 0.3 1 - 3
Gear 0.5 - 1.5 0 2 - 10
Oldham 0 1 - 5 0.5 - 1.0
Universal Joint 15 - 45 0 0

12.5.4 4. Environmental Factors

Consider operating conditions:

  • Temperature range ‚Äì Affects elastomer properties
  • Corrosive environment ‚Äì Material selection
  • Electrical isolation ‚Äì Some applications require non-conductive elements
  • Cleanliness ‚Äì Some couplings require lubrication

‚ö° Clutches

Clutches are devices that connect and disconnect rotating shafts on demand. They allow controlled engagement of power transmission.

12.5.5 Types of Clutches

Figure 12.5: Classification of common clutch types

üî¥ Friction Clutches

Friction clutches transmit torque through friction between mating surfaces. They are the most common type, allowing smooth engagement and slip during startup.

12.5.6 Single-Plate Disc Clutch

The most common design in automotive applications:

Figure 12.6: Single-plate friction clutch components

12.5.7 Friction Clutch Torque Capacity

The torque transmitted by a friction clutch depends on the clamping force and friction coefficient:

Single-surface clutch: \[T = \mu \times F \times r_m \times n\]

Where:

Symbol Description Unit
\(T\) Transmitted torque N¬∑m
\(\mu\) Coefficient of friction ‚Äî
\(F\) Axial clamping force N
\(r_m\) Mean friction radius m
\(n\) Number of friction surfaces ‚Äî

The mean friction radius can be calculated using:

Uniform pressure assumption: \[r_m = \frac{2(r_o^3 - r_i^3)}{3(r_o^2 - r_i^2)}\]

Uniform wear assumption: \[r_m = \frac{r_o + r_i}{2}\]

Where \(r_o\) is the outer radius and \(r_i\) is the inner radius of the friction surface.

12.5.8 Friction Material Properties

Table 12.2: Common Friction Material Properties
Material Œº (dry) Max Temp (¬∞C) Wear Rate Applications
Organic (woven) 0.25-0.45 250 Moderate Automotive
Organic (molded) 0.30-0.50 300 Moderate Trucks, industrial
Sintered metallic 0.25-0.35 450 Low Heavy machinery
Cerametallic 0.30-0.40 500 Low Racing, high-performance
Carbon-carbon 0.20-0.35 800+ Very Low Aircraft, F1

12.5.9 Multi-Plate Clutches

When higher torque capacity is needed in a compact space, multiple friction discs are used:

\[T = \mu \times F \times r_m \times n\]

Where \(n\) = number of friction interfaces = (number of discs √ó 2) for alternating steel and friction plates.

Example: A 5-disc clutch pack with alternating friction and steel plates has \(n = 8\) friction interfaces (4 friction discs √ó 2 sides each).

üîµ Positive Engagement Clutches

Positive engagement clutches use mechanical interlocking rather than friction. They cannot slip and require the shafts to be at rest or synchronized speed for engagement.

12.5.10 Jaw Clutch

Figure 12.7: Jaw clutch showing interlocking teeth

Characteristics:

  • No slip ‚Äì 100% efficient power transmission
  • Cannot engage at speed ‚Äì Requires synchronization
  • High torque capacity ‚Äì Limited only by tooth strength
  • Applications ‚Äì Gearbox engagement, tool spindles, marine transmissions

üü¢ Overrunning Clutches

Overrunning (one-way) clutches automatically engage in one direction and freewheel in the other. They require no external control.

Figure 12.8: Sprag-type overrunning clutch operation

12.5.11 Overrunning Clutch Applications

Application Function
Bicycle freewheel Coast without pedaling
Starter motor Disengage when engine starts
Conveyor backstop Prevent reverse motion
Indexing mechanisms Allow incremental rotation
Dual-input drives Higher-speed input overruns slower

üßÆ Worked Example: Clutch Torque Calculation

Problem: A single-plate automotive clutch has the following specifications: - Outer friction radius: 125 mm - Inner friction radius: 80 mm - Coefficient of friction: 0.35 - Axial spring force: 4500 N - Number of friction surfaces: 2 (both sides of disc contact)

Calculate the maximum torque the clutch can transmit.

Click to reveal solution

Step 1: Calculate mean friction radius (uniform wear assumption)

\[r_m = \frac{r_o + r_i}{2} = \frac{0.125 + 0.080}{2} = 0.1025 \text{ m}\]

Step 2: Apply the torque formula

\[T = \mu \times F \times r_m \times n\]

\[T = 0.35 \times 4500 \times 0.1025 \times 2\]

\[T = 322.9 \text{ N¬∑m}\]

Step 3: Verify with uniform pressure assumption (for comparison)

\[r_m = \frac{2(r_o^3 - r_i^3)}{3(r_o^2 - r_i^2)} = \frac{2(0.125^3 - 0.080^3)}{3(0.125^2 - 0.080^2)}\]

\[r_m = \frac{2(0.001953 - 0.000512)}{3(0.01563 - 0.0064)} = \frac{0.002882}{0.02769} = 0.1041 \text{ m}\]

\[T = 0.35 \times 4500 \times 0.1041 \times 2 = 328.0 \text{ N¬∑m}\]

Answer: The clutch can transmit approximately 323-328 N¬∑m depending on the pressure distribution assumption. For design purposes, use the lower (uniform wear) value with an appropriate safety factor.

Factor Recommendation

12.5.13 Clutch Selection Guidelines

Factor Recommendation
Engagement type Friction for gradual; positive for instant
Engagement frequency High cycles require better heat dissipation
Slip time Minimize to reduce heat and wear
Actuation Consider mechanical, hydraulic, pneumatic, or electromagnetic
Heat dissipation Size for thermal capacity during engagement

12.5.14 Common Problems and Solutions

Problem Likely Cause Solution
Coupling premature failure Excessive misalignment Realign shafts; select more flexible coupling
Clutch slipping Worn friction material or weak springs Replace friction disc; check spring force
Vibration Coupling imbalance or worn elements Balance or replace coupling
Noise Backlash or worn teeth (gear coupling) Replace worn components; check lubrication
Overheating Excessive slip or engagement frequency Reduce slip time; upgrade thermal capacity

üìö Chapter Summary

Couplings and clutches are essential power transmission components:

  1. Rigid couplings provide solid connections but require precise alignment

  2. Flexible couplings accommodate misalignment through elastomeric or metallic elements

  3. Misalignment types include angular, parallel (radial), and axial

  4. Friction clutches allow smooth engagement and torque control: \(T = \mu F r_m n\)

  5. Positive clutches provide no-slip engagement but require synchronization

  6. Overrunning clutches automatically engage/disengage based on direction

  7. Selection criteria include torque, speed, misalignment, environment, and cost

12.7 Practice Problems

Problem 1: A jaw coupling connects a 15 kW motor running at 1450 rpm to a pump. If the service factor is 1.5, what minimum torque rating should the coupling have?

Click for answer

Motor torque: \[T = \frac{P \times 60}{2\pi n} = \frac{15000 \times 60}{2\pi \times 1450} = 98.8 \text{ N¬∑m}\]

Required coupling torque: \[T_{coupling} = T \times SF = 98.8 \times 1.5 = 148.2 \text{ N¬∑m}\]

Select a coupling rated for at least 150 N¬∑m.

Problem 2: A multi-plate clutch has 4 friction discs and 5 steel plates (alternating). If each friction surface can transmit 45 N¬∑m, what is the total clutch torque capacity?

Click for answer

Number of friction interfaces: 4 discs √ó 2 surfaces each = 8 interfaces

Total torque capacity: \[T_{total} = 8 \times 45 = 360 \text{ N¬∑m}\]

Problem 3: A cone clutch has a mean friction radius of 75 mm, cone angle of 12¬∞, coefficient of friction 0.30, and axial force of 800 N. Calculate the torque capacity.

Click for answer

For a cone clutch, the normal force is amplified by the cone angle:

\[F_n = \frac{F_a}{\sin\alpha} = \frac{800}{\sin(12¬∞)} = \frac{800}{0.208} = 3846 \text{ N}\]

Torque: \[T = \mu \times F_n \times r_m = 0.30 \times 3846 \times 0.075 = 86.5 \text{ N¬∑m}\]

Problem 4: What type of coupling would you recommend for connecting a motor to a gearbox with 0.5 mm parallel misalignment and 0.3¬∞ angular misalignment? Justify your selection.
