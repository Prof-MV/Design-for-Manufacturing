Chapter 10 Power Transmission: Belt & Pulley Systems

ğŸ’¡ Introduction to Belt & Pulley Systems

Belt and pulley systems represent one of the oldest and most versatile methods of mechanical power transmission. From the spinning wheels of ancient civilizations to modern automotive engines and industrial machinery, belts have been transmitting power across distances for thousands of years.

Think About It: Look around your everyday environment. Your car uses multiple belt drivesâ€”the serpentine belt powers the alternator, water pump, and air conditioning compressor. A drill press uses belts to change speeds. Even household appliances like washing machines and vacuum cleaners rely on belt drives. Why do engineers choose belts over direct gear connections in so many applications?

The answer lies in the unique advantages belt drives offer:

  • Shock absorption â€“ Belts naturally dampen vibrations and load spikes
  • Distance â€“ Power can be transmitted over relatively long center distances
  • Speed flexibility â€“ Easy to change speed ratios by swapping pulleys
  • Quiet operation â€“ Much quieter than gear or chain drives
  • Overload protection â€“ Belts slip before catastrophic failure occurs
  • Low cost â€“ Generally less expensive than gear systems

âš™ï¸ Fundamental Belt Drive Concepts

10.0.1 How Belt Drives Work

A belt drive consists of at least two pulleys connected by a flexible belt. When the driving pulley (connected to the motor or power source) rotates, friction between the belt and pulley surface transmits the motion to the driven pulley.

Figure 10.1: Basic two-pulley belt drive system showing driver and driven pulleys

10.0.2 The Speed Ratio

The fundamental relationship in any belt drive is the speed ratio (also called the velocity ratio). Since the belt travels at a constant linear velocity around both pulleys:

\[v_{belt} = \omega_1 \times r_1 = \omega_2 \times r_2\]

This leads to the fundamental speed ratio equation:

\[\frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{1}{i}\]

Where:

Symbol Description Unit
\(n_1\) Rotational speed of driver pulley rpm
\(n_2\) Rotational speed of driven pulley rpm
\(D_1\) Pitch diameter of driver pulley mm or in
\(D_2\) Pitch diameter of driven pulley mm or in
\(i\) Speed ratio (transmission ratio) dimensionless

Key Insight: When the driver pulley is smaller than the driven pulley, the output speed decreases but the output torque increases proportionally. This is the principle of mechanical advantage applied to rotational systems.

ğŸ”§ Types of Belts

Different applications require different belt types. Each has distinct characteristics that make it suitable for specific uses.

10.0.4 V-Belts

V-belts are the most common type of power transmission belt. Their trapezoidal cross-section wedges into the pulley groove, creating a friction grip that increases with load.

Figure 10.3: V-belt cross-section profiles for different standard sizes

V-Belt Advantages:

  • High friction grip due to wedging action
  • Quiet operation
  • Absorbs shock loads
  • Slips before failure (overload protection)
  • Inexpensive and widely available

V-Belt Limitations:

  • Some slip and creep (typically 1-2%)
  • Not suitable for precise timing applications
  • Limited speed ratio (typically up to 6:1)
  • Temperature sensitive

10.0.5 Timing Belts (Synchronous Belts)

Timing belts have teeth that mesh with grooves in the pulley, providing positive engagement without slip. They are essential when precise speed ratios and synchronized motion are required.

Figure 10.4: Timing belt tooth profile engaging with pulley grooves

Timing Belt Advantages:

  • No slip â€“ exact speed ratio maintained
  • Can transmit high torque
  • Suitable for precision positioning
  • Long service life with proper maintenance
  • Can operate in contaminated environments

Timing Belt Applications:

  • Automotive camshaft drives (hence â€œtiming beltâ€)
  • CNC machines and 3D printers
  • Robotics and automation
  • Conveyor systems requiring precise indexing

10.0.6 Flat Belts

Flat belts are the simplest type, consisting of a rectangular cross-section. Modern flat belts often have a high-friction coating and can handle very high speeds.

Flat Belt Characteristics:

Property Rating
Speed capability Excellent (up to 50 m/s)
Power capacity Moderate
Efficiency Very high (98%+)
Noise Very quiet
Cost Low
Typical use Light-duty, high-speed applications

10.0.7 Ribbed (Poly-V) Belts

Ribbed belts combine the high-speed capability of flat belts with the grip of V-belts. Multiple small V-ribs run along the beltâ€™s inner surface.

ğŸ“Š Belt Drive Calculations

10.0.8 Speed Ratio and Output Speed

The speed ratio is the fundamental calculation for any belt drive system:

\[i = \frac{n_1}{n_2} = \frac{D_2}{D_1}\]

Rearranging to find the output speed:

\[n_2 = n_1 \times \frac{D_1}{D_2}\]

Example: A motor running at 1750 rpm drives a 100 mm pulley. The driven pulley is 250 mm. What is the output speed?

\[n_2 = 1750 \times \frac{100}{250} = 700 \text{ rpm}\]

10.0.9 Belt Length Calculation

For an open belt drive (the most common configuration), the required belt pitch length is:

\[L = 2C + \frac{\pi}{2}(D_1 + D_2) + \frac{(D_2 - D_1)^2}{4C}\]

Where:

Symbol Description Unit
\(L\) Belt pitch length mm
\(C\) Center distance between pulleys mm
\(D_1\) Pitch diameter of smaller pulley mm
\(D_2\) Pitch diameter of larger pulley mm

10.0.10 Center Distance from Belt Length

If you have a standard belt length and need to calculate the center distance:

\[C = \frac{B + \sqrt{B^2 - 8(D_2 - D_1)^2}}{8}\]

Where:

\[B = 2L - \pi(D_1 + D_2)\]

10.0.11 Arc of Contact

The arc of contact (wrap angle) on the smaller pulley is critical for power transmission:

\[\theta_1 = 180Â° - 2\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{D_2 - D_1}{2C}\right)\]

Design Rule: The arc of contact on the smaller pulley should be at least 120Â° for adequate grip. Values below this require adjustment of center distance or use of an idler pulley.

10.0.12 Belt Speed

The linear belt speed affects both power capacity and belt life:

\[v = \frac{\pi \times D \times n}{60,000}\]

Where:

Symbol Description Unit
\(v\) Belt linear velocity m/s
\(D\) Pulley pitch diameter mm
\(n\) Rotational speed rpm

Design Rule: For V-belts, optimal belt speed is typically 15-25 m/s. Speeds above 30 m/s cause excessive centrifugal force and reduce belt grip.

10.0.13 Power Transmission

The power transmitted by a belt drive depends on the tension difference between tight and slack sides:

\[P = (T_1 - T_2) \times v\]

Where:

Symbol Description Unit
\(P\) Power transmitted W
\(T_1\) Tight side tension N
\(T_2\) Slack side tension N
\(v\) Belt velocity m/s

The tension ratio is governed by the friction equation:

\[\frac{T_1}{T_2} = e^{\mu\theta}\]

Where \(\mu\) is the coefficient of friction between belt and pulley, and \(\theta\) is the arc of contact in radians.
